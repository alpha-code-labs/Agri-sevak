### Maturity Signs for Chrysanthemum

The optimal maturity stage for harvesting Chrysanthemum depends on the cultivar type (e.g., standard, spray, pompon) and the intended market (local sale, long-distance shipping). However, general signs include:

*   **Flower Development:** For most cut flower types, the flowers should be 1/2 to 3/4 open. The outer petals should be fully unfurled, and the characteristic color of the cultivar should be well-developed.
*   **Disc Florets:** For cultivars with visible disc florets (the central part of the flower), they should be just starting to show color or be slightly open, but not fully expanded.
*   **Turgidity:** Stems and flowers should be firm and turgid, indicating good hydration. Avoid harvesting wilted flowers.
*   **Foliage:** The foliage along the stem should be healthy, green, and free from pests or diseases. Lower leaves should not show signs of yellowing.
*   **Spray Types:** For spray chrysanthemums, the lead flower (terminal bud) should be open, and at least 2-3 side buds should be showing color and starting to open.

### Harvesting Time for Chrysanthemum

*   **Optimal Time of Day:** Harvesting is best done in the early morning after the dew has dried, or in the late afternoon/early evening. During these times, the plant is fully turgid, and ambient temperatures are cooler, which minimizes stress and wilting. Avoid harvesting during the hottest part of the day.
*   **Stage of Opening:**
    *   **Standard/Disbudded Types:** Harvest when the flower is 1/2 to 3/4 open. This allows the flower to fully open in the vase, providing a longer display life for the consumer.
    *   **Spray Types:** Harvest when the terminal flower is fully open and at least 2-3 lateral flowers on the spray are showing color or are partially open.
    *   **Pompon/Decorative Types:** Can often be harvested when slightly more open, as their structure is generally more robust.

### Cutting Process for Chrysanthemum

*   **Tools:** Use sharp, clean secateurs or a sharp knife. Clean tools prevent the spread of diseases and ensure a clean cut, which is crucial for water uptake.
*   **Cut Length:** Cut stems to the desired length, typically 60-90 cm, depending on the cultivar and market requirements. Ensure sufficient stem length for arranging.
*   **Angle of Cut:** Make a clean, angled cut (e.g., 45 degrees) at the base of the stem. An angled cut increases the surface area for water absorption and prevents the stem end from sitting flat on the bottom of a bucket or vase, which can block water uptake.
*   **Foliage Removal:** Immediately remove any leaves that would fall below the water line when the stems are placed in a bucket or vase. Submerged foliage decomposes rapidly, leading to bacterial growth that clogs vascular tissues and reduces water uptake, thereby shortening vase life.
*   **Immediate Hydration:** As soon as stems are cut, place them into a bucket of clean water containing a commercial floral preservative solution. This helps to rehydrate the stems, provides nutrients, and inhibits microbial growth, significantly extending the vase life.
*   **Handling:** Handle flowers carefully to avoid bruising petals or breaking stems, as damaged tissues are more susceptible to disease and premature senescence.
