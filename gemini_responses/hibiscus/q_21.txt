Namaste! As a Senior Agronomist at Haryana Agricultural University (HAU, Hisar), I understand your concern regarding nutrient toxicities in Hibiscus. While essential for plant growth, excessive levels of micronutrients like Zinc and Manganese, and macronutrients like Iron and Magnesium, can be detrimental.

It is crucial to first confirm the toxicity through **soil and plant tissue analysis**. Visual symptoms can often be misleading as they might resemble deficiencies of other nutrients or even pest/disease damage. Once confirmed, the following scientifically verified management strategies can be employed:

**General Management Principles for Nutrient Toxicity:**

1.  **Stop Application:** Immediately cease the application of any fertilizers or amendments containing the problematic nutrient.
2.  **Improve Drainage:** Poor drainage and waterlogging can exacerbate the availability and uptake of certain nutrients (especially Iron and Manganese) by creating anaerobic conditions and lowering soil pH.
3.  **Leaching:** If the soil is well-drained and water quality is good, heavy irrigation can help leach excess soluble nutrients from the root zone.
4.  **Adjust Soil pH:** Soil pH plays a critical role in nutrient availability. Raising the pH generally reduces the availability of micronutrients like Zinc, Iron, and Manganese.
5.  **Antagonistic Nutrients:** Applying certain nutrients can reduce the uptake or mitigate the effects of the toxic nutrient due to antagonistic interactions.

---

**Specific Management for Zinc (Zn) Toxicity in Hibiscus:**

*   **Symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins) on younger leaves, often resembling iron deficiency. Stunted growth, reddish-brown spots on older leaves, and necrosis (tissue death) can occur in severe cases. Root growth is also inhibited.
*   **Causes:** Over-application of zinc-containing fertilizers, use of irrigation water with high zinc content, or very acidic soil conditions (low pH) which increase zinc solubility.
*   **Management:**
    *   Stop all zinc-containing fertilizer applications.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** Apply liming materials to raise the soil pH to the optimal range for Hibiscus (typically 6.0-7.0).
        *   **Calcium Carbonate** (Agricultural Lime): Apply 500-1000 kg/acre, mixed thoroughly into the soil. The exact dose should be based on soil test recommendations.
        *   **Dolomite** (Calcium Magnesium Carbonate): If magnesium levels are also low or balanced, Dolomite can be used at similar rates.
    *   **Improve Drainage:** Ensure good soil aeration and drainage to prevent waterlogging.
    *   **Leaching:** If feasible, flush the soil with good quality water to wash out soluble zinc.
    *   **Phosphorus Application:** If soil phosphorus levels are low, a moderate application of phosphorus fertilizer can help reduce zinc availability by forming insoluble zinc phosphates.
        *   **Single Super Phosphate (SSP)**: Apply 100-200 kg/acre, based on soil test.

---

**Specific Management for Iron (Fe) Toxicity in Hibiscus:**

*   **Symptoms:** Bronzing or purpling of older leaves, followed by necrosis (blackening/browning) starting from the leaf tips and margins. Stunted growth and reduced root development are also common.
*   **Causes:** Very acidic soil conditions (low pH), waterlogging or poor drainage (which creates anaerobic conditions and increases iron solubility), or excessive application of iron fertilizers.
*   **Management:**
    *   Stop all iron-containing fertilizer applications.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** Raise the soil pH to the optimal range for Hibiscus (6.0-7.0).
        *   **Calcium Carbonate** (Agricultural Lime): Apply 500-1000 kg/acre, mixed thoroughly into the soil.
        *   **Dolomite** (Calcium Magnesium Carbonate): Use at similar rates if magnesium is also needed.
    *   **Improve Drainage and Aeration:** This is critical. Ensure the soil is well-drained and not waterlogged. Improve soil structure by incorporating organic matter.
    *   **Avoid Waterlogging:** Do not overwater the plants, especially in heavy soils.

---

**Specific Management for Magnesium (Mg) Toxicity in Hibiscus:**

*   **Symptoms:** Magnesium toxicity is less common than deficiency. It can induce deficiencies of other essential nutrients like Calcium (Ca) and Potassium (K) due to antagonistic interactions. Symptoms may include general stunting, marginal chlorosis, leaf tip burn, and reduced overall growth.
*   **Causes:** Excessive application of magnesium fertilizers (e.g., Epsom salts), high magnesium content in irrigation water, or an imbalanced nutrient ratio (e.g., very high Mg:Ca or Mg:K ratio in the soil).
*   **Management:**
    *   Stop all magnesium-containing fertilizer applications.
    *   **Leaching:** Flush the soil with good quality water to remove excess soluble magnesium.
    *   **Improve Ca:Mg Ratio:** Apply calcium-containing amendments to balance the Ca:Mg ratio.
        *   **Calcium Carbonate** (Agricultural Lime): Apply 200-500 kg/acre, if soil pH needs to be raised.
        *   **Gypsum** (Calcium Sulfate): Apply 200-500 kg/acre, if soil pH is already adequate but calcium is needed.
    *   **Improve K:Mg Ratio:** If potassium levels are low, apply potassium fertilizers to balance the K:Mg ratio.
        *   **Potassium Chloride** (Muriate of Potash): Apply 50-100 kg/acre, based on soil test.
        *   **Potassium Sulfate** (Sulfate of Potash): Apply 50-100 kg/acre, based on soil test.

---

**Specific Management for Manganese (Mn) Toxicity in Hibiscus:**

*   **Symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis on younger leaves, often accompanied by small dark brown or black necrotic spots on leaves, especially older ones. Leaves may appear crinkled or cupped. Stunted growth and reduced root development are common. Manganese toxicity can also induce iron deficiency.
*   **Causes:** Very acidic soil conditions (low pH), waterlogging, or excessive application of manganese fertilizers.
*   **Management:**
    *   Stop all manganese-containing fertilizer applications.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** Raise the soil pH to the optimal range for Hibiscus (6.0-7.0).
        *   **Calcium Carbonate** (Agricultural Lime): Apply 500-1000 kg/acre, mixed thoroughly into the soil.
        *   **Dolomite** (Calcium Magnesium Carbonate): Use at similar rates if magnesium is also needed.
    *   **Improve Drainage and Aeration:** Ensure good soil aeration and drainage to prevent waterlogging, which increases manganese solubility.
    *   **Leaching:** Flush the soil with good quality water to remove soluble manganese.
    *   **Calcium Application:** Increasing soil calcium levels can help reduce manganese uptake.
        *   **Calcium Carbonate** or **Gypsum**: As mentioned above for pH adjustment or calcium supply.

---

Always remember that prevention is better than cure. Regular soil testing and balanced fertilization based on crop requirements are key to avoiding nutrient toxicities. For precise recommendations tailored to your specific farm conditions, please consult with the HAU extension services or a qualified agronomist after obtaining a detailed soil analysis report.
