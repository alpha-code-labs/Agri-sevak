### Control of Physical Damage in Acid Lime

**1. Fruit Bursting (Cracking/Splitting)**

This condition is primarily a physiological disorder caused by rapid fluctuations in soil moisture, especially when a dry spell is followed by heavy irrigation or rainfall. Nutritional imbalances, particularly deficiencies of Boron and Calcium, can also contribute by affecting rind elasticity.

*   **Causes:**
    *   Sudden changes in soil moisture (dry to wet).
    *   High temperatures and low humidity.
    *   Deficiency of Boron and Calcium.
*   **Control Measures:**
    *   **Consistent Water Management:** Maintain uniform soil moisture throughout the fruit development period. Avoid extremes of drought and waterlogging. Drip irrigation is highly recommended to ensure precise and consistent water delivery.
    *   **Mulching:** Apply organic mulch (e.g., straw, dry leaves, sugarcane trash) around the base of the trees. This helps conserve soil moisture, regulate soil temperature, and reduce evaporation.
    *   **Nutrient Management:**
        *   **Boron:** Apply foliar spray of **Boric acid** at 1-2 g/L of water (0.1-0.2%) during fruit development stages. Alternatively, soil application of **Borax** can be done based on soil test recommendations.
        *   **Calcium:** Apply foliar spray of **Calcium chloride** at 5-10 g/L of water (0.5-1.0%) during fruit growth. Soil application of **Gypsum** can also improve soil calcium levels over time.

**2. Leaf Burning/Melting**

This refers to symptoms like scorching, browning, or desiccation of leaf tissue, often due to environmental stress or improper chemical application.

*   **Causes:**
    *   **Heat Stress/Sunburn:** Intense solar radiation and high temperatures, especially during peak summer months.
    *   **Water Stress:** Both severe drought (leading to desiccation) and waterlogging (damaging roots and impairing water uptake).
    *   **Chemical Phytotoxicity:** Improper application of pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers (e.g., high concentration, application during hot weather).
    *   **Nutrient Toxicity:** Excess salts in soil or irrigation water, or specific nutrient toxicities (e.g., Boron toxicity).
*   **Control Measures:**
    *   **Adequate and Consistent Irrigation:** Ensure the plants receive sufficient water, especially during hot and dry periods. Avoid both underwatering and overwatering.
    *   **Shade Management:** Provide partial shade during the hottest parts of the day in peak summer. Shade nets (e.g., 30-50% shade) can be effective for young plants or in areas with extreme heat.
    *   **Mulching:** Helps regulate soil temperature and moisture, reducing stress on roots.
    *   **Proper Chemical Application:** Always follow recommended dosages and application timings for all agrochemicals. Avoid spraying during high temperatures or strong sunlight to prevent phytotoxicity. Use appropriate spray equipment and ensure uniform coverage.
    *   **Windbreaks:** Plant windbreaks around the orchard to protect trees from hot, dry winds that can exacerbate desiccation.
    *   **Soil and Water Testing:** Regularly test soil and irrigation water to identify and address any nutrient toxicities or salinity issues.

**3. Hardness in Fruit**

"Hardness" in fruit can refer to a lack of juiciness, thick rind, or a generally firm texture that is undesirable for consumption. This is often linked to physiological factors rather than a specific disease.

*   **Causes:**
    *   **Water Stress:** Insufficient or inconsistent water supply during fruit development.
    *   **Nutrient Imbalance:** Particularly low Potassium levels and sometimes excessive Nitrogen can lead to thicker rinds and reduced juiciness.
    *   **Premature Harvesting:** Harvesting fruit before it reaches optimal maturity.
    *   **High Temperatures:** Can sometimes lead to reduced juice content.
*   **Control Measures:**
    *   **Consistent Irrigation:** Ensure a steady and adequate supply of water throughout the fruit development and ripening stages. This is crucial for cell expansion and juice formation.
    *   **Balanced Nutrient Management:**
        *   Ensure adequate **Potassium** supply, which is vital for fruit quality, size, and juiciness. Apply **Muriate of Potash** (Potassium chloride) or **Sulphate of Potash** (Potassium sulphate) as per soil test recommendations.
        *   Avoid excessive **Nitrogen** application, which can promote vegetative growth at the expense of fruit quality and may lead to thicker rinds.
    *   **Optimal Harvesting:** Harvest fruits at their physiological maturity stage when they have developed their characteristic color, size, and juiciness. Harvesting too early will result in hard, less juicy fruits.
    *   **Mulching:** Helps maintain stable soil moisture and temperature, contributing to better fruit development and quality.
