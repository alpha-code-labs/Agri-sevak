### **I. Improving Yield**

Optimizing yield involves a holistic approach focusing on plant health, nutrition, and proper cultural practices.

1.  **Balanced Nutrition:**
    *   **Soil Testing:** Conduct regular soil tests to determine nutrient deficiencies and apply fertilizers accordingly.
    *   **Macronutrients:** Ensure adequate supply of Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K). A general recommendation for mature trees (5+ years) might be around 200-300 g N, 100-150 g P2O5, and 200-300 g K2O per tree per year, adjusted based on soil test and tree vigor.
    *   **Micronutrients:**
        *   **Boron:** Crucial for pollen viability and fruit set. Apply **Borax** (Sodium tetraborate decahydrate) as a foliar spray at 1-2 g/L (0.1-0.2%) before flowering and again at fruit set.
        *   **Zinc:** Essential for enzyme activity and growth. Apply **Zinc EDTA** as a foliar spray at 0.5 g/L (0.05%) during new flush growth.

2.  **Efficient Irrigation:**
    *   Maintain consistent soil moisture, especially during critical stages like flowering, fruit set, and rapid fruit development. Water stress during these periods can lead to flower drop, poor fruit set, and reduced fruit size. Drip irrigation is highly recommended for water efficiency.

3.  **Pollination Management:**
    *   Ensure adequate bee activity during flowering. Place 4-5 bee colonies per acre in the orchard during bloom.

4.  **Pruning and Training:**
    *   **Dormant Pruning:** Annually prune during dormancy to remove dead, diseased, or crossing branches, and to maintain an open canopy for better light penetration and air circulation, which promotes fruit bud formation.
    *   **Summer Pruning:** Light summer pruning can help manage canopy density and direct energy towards fruit development.

5.  **Fruit Thinning:**
    *   Thinning is crucial to reduce competition among fruits, leading to larger, higher-quality fruits and preventing biennial bearing.
    *   **Manual Thinning:** Thin fruits manually when they are 1.5-2.5 cm in diameter, leaving fruits 10-15 cm apart on the branch.
    *   **Chemical Thinning:**
        *   **Naphthalene Acetic Acid (NAA):** Apply at 5-10 mg/L (ppm) 10-15 days after full bloom. Timing is critical; consult local HAU experts for precise timing based on variety and environmental conditions.

### **II. Improving Fruit Quality**

Fruit quality encompasses size, color, flavor, firmness, and sugar content.

1.  **Optimized Nutrient Management:**
    *   **Potassium:** Plays a vital role in sugar accumulation, color development, and overall fruit flavor. Apply **Potassium sulphate** as a foliar spray at 10-20 g/L (1-2%) during the fruit development stage (pit hardening to final swell).
    *   **Calcium:** Essential for cell wall integrity, leading to firmer fruits and reduced physiological disorders. Apply **Calcium nitrate** as a foliar spray at 5-10 g/L (0.5-1%) during fruit growth, with 2-3 applications.

2.  **Adequate Sunlight Exposure:**
    *   Proper pruning ensures good light penetration into the canopy, which is essential for uniform fruit color development and sugar synthesis.

3.  **Water Management:**
    *   Avoid water stress or excessive irrigation during the final stages of fruit ripening, as both can negatively impact fruit quality (e.g., bland taste, cracking).

4.  **Plant Growth Regulators (PGRs):**
    *   **Ethephon:** For uniform ripening and enhanced color development. Apply **Ethephon** at 100-200 mg/L (ppm) 7-10 days before the anticipated harvest date. This helps in achieving a more uniform harvest of ripe, well-colored fruits.

### **III. Improving Storage Life**

Extending storage life requires attention to pre-harvest practices, harvest maturity, and post-harvest handling.

1.  **Pre-harvest Factors:**
    *   **Calcium Application:** Continue foliar applications of **Calcium chloride** at 5-10 g/L (0.5-1%) during fruit development, with 2-3 sprays, up to 2-3 weeks before harvest. Calcium strengthens cell walls, reducing softening and susceptibility to decay during storage.
    *   **Pest and Disease Control:** Ensure the orchard is free from pests and diseases, as damaged or infected fruits have significantly reduced storage potential.
    *   **Harvest Maturity:** Harvest peaches at the optimal maturity stage – when they have reached full size, developed characteristic color, but are still firm. Over-ripe fruits have very short storage life.
    *   **Aminoethoxyvinylglycine (AVG):** To delay ripening and extend on-tree and post-harvest life. Apply **Aminoethoxyvinylglycine** at 125 mg/L (ppm) 7-14 days before the anticipated harvest.

2.  **Post-harvest Handling:**
    *   **Gentle Handling:** Minimize bruising and mechanical damage during harvesting, sorting, and packing, as damaged fruits are highly susceptible to decay.
    *   **Rapid Pre-cooling:** Immediately after harvest, cool the fruits rapidly to their optimum storage temperature (0-2°C) using hydro-cooling or forced-air cooling. This removes field heat and slows down respiration and ripening.
    *   **Fungicide Treatment:** To control post-harvest rots.
        *   Dip fruits for 1-2 minutes in a solution of **Carbendazim** at 0.5 g/L (0.05%) or **Thiophanate-methyl** at 0.7 g/L (0.07%) immediately after harvest and pre-cooling. Ensure the solution is fresh and maintained at the correct concentration.
    *   **1-Methylcyclopropene (1-MCP) Treatment:**
        *   **1-Methylcyclopropene (1-MCP)** is a potent ethylene action inhibitor. Treat fruits with 1-MCP in an enclosed space (e.g., cold room) for 12-24 hours immediately after harvest and pre-cooling. The concentration and duration depend on the specific product formulation and fruit variety; follow manufacturer's guidelines. This significantly delays ripening and senescence.
    *   **Storage Conditions:** Store peaches at 0-2°C with 90-95% relative humidity. Controlled Atmosphere (CA) storage, if available, can further extend storage life by reducing oxygen and increasing carbon dioxide levels.
