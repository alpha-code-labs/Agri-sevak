**Leaf Burning/Melting in Tobacco**

Leaf burning or scorching in tobacco is a physiological disorder, not a disease caused by pathogens. It can manifest as browning, drying, or necrosis of leaf tissue, often starting at the margins or tips. "Melting" implies severe cellular collapse, usually due to extreme heat or chemical damage.

**Common Causes:**

1.  **Sun Scorch/Heat Stress:** Intense sunlight combined with high temperatures, especially when plants are under water stress, can cause leaf tissue to burn.
2.  **Chemical Phytotoxicity:** Improper application of pesticides, herbicides, or even foliar fertilizers (incorrect concentration, application during hot periods) can lead to chemical burn.
3.  **Water Stress:** Both severe water deficit (drought) and waterlogging (leading to root damage and impaired water uptake) can cause leaf margins to scorch.
4.  **Nutrient Imbalances:**
    *   **Toxicity:** Excessive levels of certain nutrients (e.g., boron, chloride) can cause marginal leaf burn.
    *   **Deficiency:** Severe deficiencies, particularly of potassium, can make leaves more susceptible to scorching under stress conditions.
5.  **Air Pollution:** High levels of certain air pollutants (e.g., ozone) can cause leaf damage resembling burning.

**Control Measures for Leaf Burning/Melting:**

Control focuses primarily on preventative cultural practices and proper management to reduce plant stress.

*   **1. Optimal Water Management:**
    *   Ensure consistent and adequate irrigation, especially during dry spells and peak heat periods. Avoid both drought stress and waterlogging.
    *   Irrigate in the early morning or late evening to minimize evaporation and allow plants to absorb water before the hottest part of the day.
    *   Use mulching (e.g., crop residues) around the base of plants to conserve soil moisture and moderate soil temperature.

*   **2. Balanced Nutrient Management:**
    *   Conduct regular soil testing to determine nutrient status and pH.
    *   Apply fertilizers based on HAU recommendations for tobacco, ensuring a balanced supply of macro and micronutrients.
    *   Avoid excessive application of nitrogen, which can lead to lush, tender growth more susceptible to scorching.
    *   If potassium deficiency is identified or suspected to contribute to stress susceptibility, apply potassium-containing fertilizers to the soil:
        *   **Potassium sulfate** (e.g., 50-100 kg/acre, based on soil test)
        *   **Potassium chloride** (e.g., 40-80 kg/acre, based on soil test)
    *   Foliar application of **Potassium nitrate** (e.g., 0.5-1.0% solution, 5-10 g/L water) can be considered during periods of high stress to improve plant resilience, but always test on a small area first and apply during cooler parts of the day.

*   **3. Prevent Chemical Phytotoxicity:**
    *   Always read and strictly follow the label instructions for all agrochemicals (pesticides, herbicides, foliar fertilizers) regarding dosage, application method, and environmental conditions.
    *   Avoid spraying during the hottest part of the day or when plants are under severe water stress.
    *   Ensure spray equipment is calibrated correctly to avoid over-application.

*   **4. Shade Management (for nurseries/young plants):**
    *   In nurseries, use shade nets (e.g., 30-50% shade) during intense summer months to protect young seedlings from direct, harsh sunlight.

*   **5. Appropriate Planting Time:**
    *   Adjust planting schedules to ensure critical growth stages do not coincide with the most extreme heat or drought periods, based on local climatic data for Haryana.

By implementing these scientifically verified practices, you can significantly reduce the incidence of leaf burning/melting in your tobacco crop.
