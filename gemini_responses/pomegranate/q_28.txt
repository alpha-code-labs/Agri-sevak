### Protection from Extreme Cold and Frost

Pomegranate plants, especially young ones, are susceptible to frost damage. While mature trees can tolerate temperatures down to -10Â°C for short durations, prolonged exposure or sudden temperature drops can cause significant damage to shoots, flowers, and young fruits.

*   **Site Selection:** Whenever possible, choose elevated sites or areas with good air drainage for new plantings. Cold air is denser and settles in low-lying areas, creating frost pockets.
*   **Pre-Frost Irrigation:** Apply a light irrigation to the orchard a day or two before an anticipated frost event. Moist soil retains heat better than dry soil, radiating it slowly through the night and keeping the ambient temperature slightly higher.
*   **Mulching:** Apply a thick layer (10-15 cm) of organic mulch (e.g., straw, dry leaves, sugarcane trash) around the base of young plants. This insulates the soil, protects the root zone from freezing, and helps retain soil warmth.
*   **Covering Young Plants:** For young plants (1-3 years old), individual covering with hessian cloth, straw, or non-woven fabric can provide significant protection during severe frost nights. Ensure the cover reaches the ground to trap warmth effectively.
*   **Windbreaks:** Plant shelterbelts or windbreaks (e.g., trees like Eucalyptus, Poplar) on the northern and western sides of the orchard to reduce the impact of cold, dry winds.
*   **Foliar Sprays:**
    *   **Potassium silicate:** A foliar spray of **Potassium silicate** (2-3 ml/L water) can help strengthen cell walls and improve the plant's tolerance to cold stress. Apply before the anticipated onset of cold weather.

### Protection from Heat (Loo) and Intense Sunlight

High temperatures, hot dry winds (locally known as 'Loo'), and intense sunlight during summer months in Haryana can cause sunburn on fruits and branches, leaf scorching, reduced fruit set, and overall decline in fruit quality and yield.

*   **Adequate Irrigation:** Maintain optimal soil moisture levels, especially during hot periods. Frequent, light irrigation is more effective than infrequent heavy irrigation during heat waves. Drip irrigation is highly recommended for efficient water use.
*   **Mulching:** Apply organic mulch (10-15 cm) around the base of trees to conserve soil moisture, reduce soil temperature, and suppress weed growth, all of which contribute to better heat stress management.
*   **Whitewashing/Reflective Sprays:**
    *   **Kaolin clay:** Spraying the trunk, main branches, and even the fruit with a solution of **Kaolin clay** (50-75 g/L water) creates a white, reflective film. This reflects sunlight, significantly reduces the surface temperature of bark and fruit, and minimizes sunburn and heat stress. Reapply after heavy rains or as needed.
    *   **Lime Wash:** A simple lime wash (calcium carbonate) can also be applied to trunks and main branches for similar reflective benefits, protecting the bark from sunscald.
*   **Shade Nets:** For young plants or during periods of extreme heat waves, temporary installation of 25-30% shade nets over the orchard can significantly reduce heat stress and prevent sunburn on fruits.
*   **Windbreaks:** Establish shelterbelts on the western and southern sides of the orchard to protect plants from hot, desiccating winds (Loo), which exacerbate heat stress.
*   **Canopy Management:** Prune trees to develop a balanced canopy that provides some self-shading to the fruits without excessively reducing air circulation. Avoid severe pruning during hot months, as this can expose fruits and branches to direct sunlight.
*   **Foliar Sprays:**
    *   **Potassium silicate:** A foliar spray of **Potassium silicate** (2-3 ml/L water) can enhance the plant's ability to withstand heat stress by improving cell wall integrity and reducing water loss through transpiration.
