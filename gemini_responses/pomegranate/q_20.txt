Namaste! As a Senior Agronomist at Haryana Agricultural University (HAU, Hisar), I understand the critical role of micronutrients in achieving optimal yield and quality in Pomegranate cultivation. Addressing these deficiencies scientifically is key.

Before any application, it is highly recommended to conduct a comprehensive soil test and, if possible, a leaf tissue analysis. This will accurately diagnose the specific deficiencies and their severity, allowing for precise and efficient nutrient management. Over-application of micronutrients can be detrimental.

Here's how to address the common micronutrient deficiencies in Pomegranate:

### General Recommendations for Micronutrient Application:

*   **Foliar Application:** Generally preferred for quick correction of deficiencies, especially for Iron and Zinc, as it bypasses soil fixation issues common in Haryana's alkaline soils. Apply sprays during cooler parts of the day (morning or late afternoon) to maximize absorption and minimize leaf burn.
*   **Soil Application:** Suitable for long-term maintenance and for nutrients like Boron and Calcium, but requires careful monitoring to avoid toxicity.

---

### 1. Zinc (Zn) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Small, narrow leaves (little leaf), interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins) on younger leaves, rosetting of leaves, reduced fruit size and quality.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray:**
        *   **Zinc Sulphate Heptahydrate** (21% Zn) @ 5 g/L of water.
        *   **Zinc EDTA** (12% Zn) @ 1 g/L of water.
        *   Apply 2-3 sprays at 15-20 day intervals, starting from new flush emergence.
    *   **Soil Application:**
        *   **Zinc Sulphate Monohydrate** (33% Zn) @ 5-10 kg/acre, broadcast or band applied, once a year before flowering.

### 2. Iron (Fe) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Pronounced interveinal chlorosis on young leaves, turning almost white in severe cases, while veins remain green. Often seen in high pH (alkaline) soils, common in Haryana.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray (Highly Recommended for Haryana soils):**
        *   **Ferrous Sulphate** (19% Fe) @ 5 g/L of water.
        *   **Iron EDTA** (12% Fe) @ 1 g/L of water.
        *   Apply 2-3 sprays at 10-15 day intervals, especially on new flushes. Repeat as needed.
    *   **Soil Application:** Less effective in high pH soils due to rapid fixation. If applied, use **Ferrous Sulphate** @ 20-30 kg/acre, but foliar is generally superior.

### 3. Boron (B) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Cracking of fruits, internal browning or corky spots in fruits, reduced fruit set, dieback of growing points, thickened and brittle leaves.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray:**
        *   **Disodium Octaborate Tetrahydrate** (20% B) @ 1-1.5 g/L of water.
        *   **Borax** (Sodium Tetraborate Decahydrate, 10.5-11.3% B) @ 2 g/L of water.
        *   Apply at pre-flowering and during early fruit development stages.
    *   **Soil Application:**
        *   **Borax** (Sodium Tetraborate Decahydrate) @ 2-4 kg/acre, once a year.
        *   **Caution:** Boron has a narrow range between deficiency and toxicity. Over-application can severely damage the crop. Always base soil application on a soil test report.

### 4. Calcium (Ca) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Poor fruit quality, reduced shelf life, sometimes blossom end rot (though less common in pomegranate than other fruits), tip burn on young leaves, stunted growth.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray:**
        *   **Calcium Nitrate** (18.8% Ca) @ 5-10 g/L of water.
        *   **Calcium Chloride** (27% Ca) @ 5 g/L of water.
        *   Apply during fruit development stages, 2-3 sprays at 15-day intervals.
    *   **Soil Application:**
        *   **Gypsum** (Calcium Sulphate Dihydrate) @ 200-400 kg/acre, depending on soil type and pH, can be incorporated into the soil. This also helps in improving soil structure.
        *   **Calcium Nitrate** can also be applied through fertigation.

### 5. Magnesium (Mg) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis on older leaves, starting from the leaf margins and progressing inwards, leaves may turn reddish-purple in severe cases.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray:**
        *   **Magnesium Sulphate** (9.6% Mg) @ 5-10 g/L of water.
        *   Apply 2-3 sprays at 15-20 day intervals, especially when symptoms appear.
    *   **Soil Application:**
        *   **Magnesium Sulphate** @ 10-20 kg/acre, once a year, can be broadcast or band applied.

### 6. Manganese (Mn) Deficiency

*   **Symptoms:** Interveinal chlorosis on younger leaves, similar to iron deficiency but often less severe, sometimes with small necrotic spots.
*   **Correction:**
    *   **Foliar Spray:**
        *   **Manganese Sulphate** (28-30% Mn) @ 2.5-5 g/L of water.
        *   **Manganese EDTA** (12% Mn) @ 1 g/L of water.
        *   Apply 2-3 sprays at 15-20 day intervals.
    *   **Soil Application:**
        *   **Manganese Sulphate** @ 5-10 kg/acre, once a year.

---

**Important Considerations:**

*   **Water Quality:** Ensure the water used for foliar sprays has a neutral pH (6.0-7.0) for optimal nutrient absorption.
*   **Adjuvants:** Adding a good quality non-ionic spreader/sticker to foliar sprays can improve coverage and absorption.
*   **Integrated Nutrient Management:** Micronutrient application should be part of a holistic nutrient management plan, including balanced NPK fertilization and organic matter incorporation.
*   **Consultation:** For specific recommendations tailored to your farm's conditions, please consult with HAU experts or your local agricultural extension officer after obtaining soil and tissue test reports.

By following these scientifically verified guidelines, you can effectively manage micronutrient deficiencies in your Pomegranate orchard and ensure healthy, productive trees.
