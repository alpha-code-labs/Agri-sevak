Here is the information regarding the practices you inquired about:

*   **Pruning:**
    This is not known to affect Cauliflower. Cauliflower is a non-branching, single-stemmed crop grown for its compact, immature flower head (curd). Pruning practices, such as removing side shoots or apical dominance, are not applicable or beneficial for commercial cauliflower production.

*   **Desuckering:**
    This is not known to affect Cauliflower. Cauliflower does not produce suckers in a manner that requires desuckering for improved yield or quality of the curd. The plant focuses its energy on developing a single, central curd.

*   **Mulching:**
    Mulching is a highly beneficial specialized practice for cauliflower cultivation, especially in Haryana's climate. It helps in:
    *   **Moisture Conservation:** Reduces evaporation from the soil surface, conserving irrigation water.
    *   **Weed Control:** Suppresses weed growth, reducing competition for nutrients and water.
    *   **Soil Temperature Regulation:** Keeps the soil cooler in hot periods and warmer in cold periods, providing a more stable root environment.
    *   **Curd Quality:** Helps in maintaining curd whiteness by preventing direct sunlight exposure (though blanching is also done).
    *   **Soil Health:** Organic mulches decompose over time, adding organic matter to the soil.

    **Recommended Mulching Materials:**
    *   **Plastic Mulch:** Black or white-on-black polyethylene film (25-100 micron thickness) is very effective. Black mulch is preferred for weed control and soil warming in cooler seasons, while white-on-black can help reflect heat in hotter periods.
        *   **Application:** Lay the plastic film over prepared beds before transplanting, making holes for the seedlings.
    *   **Organic Mulches:** Crop residues like wheat straw, paddy straw, sugarcane trash, or dried leaves can be used.
        *   **Application:** Apply a layer of 5-10 cm thickness around the base of the plants after transplanting.
        *   **Quantity:** Approximately 4-6 tonnes per acre of straw mulch is generally sufficient.

*   **Grafting:**
    This is not known to affect Cauliflower. Grafting is not a standard or common practice for commercial cauliflower production. Cauliflower is typically grown directly from seeds, and there are no widely adopted rootstocks for grafting cauliflower to impart disease resistance or enhance vigor, unlike in some other vegetable crops.

*   **Pollination:**
    This is not known to affect Cauliflower as a specialized practice for curd production. Cauliflower is cultivated for its vegetative curd (immature flower head), which is harvested before the plant flowers. Therefore, pollination is not a concern or a specialized practice for commercial curd production. If cauliflower is left to bolt and produce seeds, it is primarily self-pollinating, though insects can aid in cross-pollination. However, this is for seed production, not for the edible curd.
