It is important to clarify that while you've mentioned several types of rust, most of them are not known to affect Marigold.

*   **Black Rust (Stem Rust), Brown Rust (Leaf Rust), and Yellow Rust (Stripe Rust)** are primarily diseases of cereal crops like wheat and barley. **This is not known to affect Marigold.**
*   **White Rust** is caused by *Albugo candida* and primarily affects cruciferous crops (e.g., mustard, radish). **This is not known to affect Marigold.**
*   **Red Rust** is a general term and if referring to specific *Puccinia* species, it is generally not associated with Marigold. **This is not known to affect Marigold.**

The primary rust disease affecting Marigold (Tagetes spp.) is **Marigold Rust**, caused by the fungus *Puccinia tageticola*. This is the disease we need to focus on controlling.

Here's an integrated approach to manage Marigold Rust:

**I. Cultural and Preventive Measures (First Line of Defense):**

1.  **Resistant Varieties:** Where available, choose Marigold varieties known to have resistance or tolerance to rust. Consult local nurseries or HAU for recommendations suitable for Haryana.
2.  **Sanitation:**
    *   Remove and destroy infected plant parts (leaves, stems) as soon as symptoms appear to reduce the source of inoculum.
    *   Clear plant debris from previous Marigold crops, as spores can overwinter in plant residues.
3.  **Proper Spacing:** Ensure adequate spacing between plants to promote good air circulation. This helps to dry leaf surfaces quickly, making conditions less favourable for fungal spore germination and infection.
4.  **Watering Practices:**
    *   Avoid overhead irrigation, especially late in the day, which keeps foliage wet for extended periods.
    *   Water plants at the base or use drip irrigation. If overhead irrigation is unavoidable, do it in the morning so leaves can dry quickly in the sun.
5.  **Crop Rotation:** Avoid planting Marigolds in the same spot year after year. Rotate with non-host crops to break the disease cycle.
6.  **Nutrient Management:** Maintain balanced plant nutrition. Over-fertilization with nitrogen can lead to lush, soft growth that is more susceptible to diseases.

**II. Chemical Control (When Cultural Practices are Insufficient):**

Fungicides should be applied preventively or at the very first sign of disease symptoms for best efficacy. Always ensure thorough coverage of the foliage. Rotate fungicides with different modes of action to prevent the development of resistance.

Here are some CIBRC approved technical chemical names effective against rusts, along with their typical dosages for foliar application:

*   **Propiconazole** (25% EC): Apply at 0.5 - 1 ml per litre of water.
*   **Tebuconazole** (25.9% EC): Apply at 0.75 - 1 ml per litre of water.
*   **Difenoconazole** (25% EC): Apply at 0.5 ml per litre of water.
*   **Azoxystrobin** (23% SC): Apply at 0.5 - 1 ml per litre of water.
*   **Mancozeb** (75% WP): Apply at 2 - 2.5 g per litre of water. (This is a contact fungicide, good for preventive application or in rotation).

**Important Considerations:**

*   Always read and follow the instructions on the fungicide label carefully, including dosage, application timing, safety precautions, and pre-harvest intervals.
*   Ensure proper protective equipment (gloves, mask, eye protection) is used during mixing and application.
*   Consult with your local agricultural extension officer or HAU experts for specific recommendations tailored to your farm's conditions and the current disease pressure in your area.

By implementing these integrated management strategies, you can effectively control Marigold Rust and ensure healthy Marigold production.
