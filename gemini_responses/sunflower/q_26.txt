Here's information on the requested practices:

*   **Pruning:**
    This is not a standard commercial practice for sunflower cultivation. Sunflowers are typically grown as a single-stemmed annual crop. While some varieties may produce lateral branches, their removal (which could be considered a form of pruning) is generally not recommended as it does not consistently lead to increased yield and can sometimes reduce it by removing potential capitula (flower heads).

*   **Desuckering:**
    This is not known to affect Sunflower. Sunflowers do not typically produce suckers from the base of the plant in the way some other crops (e.g., sugarcane, maize in some contexts) do. Therefore, desuckering is not a practice employed in sunflower cultivation.

*   **Mulching:**
    Mulching can be a beneficial practice in sunflower cultivation, particularly in regions like Haryana where moisture conservation and weed management are crucial.
    *   **Benefits:**
        *   **Moisture Conservation:** Reduces evaporation from the soil surface, conserving soil moisture, which is vital during dry spells.
        *   **Weed Suppression:** Acts as a physical barrier, suppressing weed growth and reducing competition for nutrients and water.
        *   **Soil Temperature Regulation:** Helps to moderate soil temperature, keeping it cooler in hot weather and warmer in cooler periods.
        *   **Soil Health Improvement:** Organic mulches decompose over time, adding organic matter to the soil and improving its structure and fertility.
    *   **Materials:** Common mulching materials include crop residues (e.g., wheat straw, rice straw), dry leaves, or even plastic mulches (though less common for large-scale sunflower).
    *   **Application:** A layer of 5-10 cm of organic mulch can be applied around the base of the plants after establishment.

*   **Grafting:**
    This is not known to affect Sunflower. Sunflowers are herbaceous annual plants and are not propagated or grown commercially using grafting techniques. Grafting is typically reserved for perennial crops, fruit trees, or certain vegetables to combine desirable rootstock and scion characteristics.

*   **Pollination:**
    Pollination is a critical process for successful seed set and yield in sunflowers.
    *   **Mechanism:** Sunflowers are primarily cross-pollinated (allogamous) by insects, especially bees (Apis mellifera and other wild bees), but they also exhibit a degree of self-pollination (autogamy).
    *   **Importance of Bees:** Insect pollination significantly enhances seed set, seed weight, and oil content. The presence of a healthy bee population in and around sunflower fields is highly beneficial.
    *   **Self-Pollination:** While self-pollination can occur, especially in certain varieties, it often results in lower seed set and quality compared to cross-pollination.
    *   **Management:** To ensure good pollination, farmers are encouraged to:
        *   Avoid spraying insecticides during the flowering period, especially when bees are active. If spraying is unavoidable, use bee-safe insecticides and apply them in the late evening or early morning when bees are not foraging.
        *   Consider placing bee colonies (apiaries) near sunflower fields, particularly for hybrid seed production. A density of 2-3 bee colonies per acre (5-7 colonies per hectare) is often recommended for optimal pollination.
