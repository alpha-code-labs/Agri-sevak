Regarding the conditions you've mentioned:

*   **Fruit bursting:** This is not known to affect Sugarcane. Sugarcane is a grass and its economic product is the stalk (stem), not a fruit in the botanical sense that would burst.
*   **Hardness in fruit:** This is not known to affect Sugarcane. As mentioned, sugarcane produces stalks, not fruits. Stalk hardness (fibrousness) is a quality parameter influenced by variety, maturity, and environmental factors, but it is not considered a "physical damage" in the context of fruit hardness.
*   **Leaf burning/melting:** "Leaf melting" is not a typical term used to describe damage in sugarcane. However, "leaf burning" or leaf scorch can occur in sugarcane due to various environmental stresses or chemical phytotoxicity. This is primarily a symptom of stress rather than a direct physical damage in the sense of mechanical injury.

**Control Measures for Leaf Burning/Scorch in Sugarcane (Primarily Cultural and Preventative):**

Leaf burning or scorch in sugarcane can be caused by several factors, including extreme temperatures, water stress, nutrient deficiencies, and chemical phytotoxicity. The control measures focus on managing these underlying causes:

*   **Variety Selection:**
    *   Choose sugarcane varieties that are known to be tolerant to prevailing environmental stresses in your region, such as heat, drought, or frost.
*   **Irrigation Management:**
    *   Ensure adequate and timely irrigation, especially during critical growth stages and dry periods, to prevent water stress. Proper irrigation scheduling based on soil type and weather conditions is crucial.
*   **Nutrient Management:**
    *   Conduct regular soil testing to determine nutrient status.
    *   Apply balanced fertilizers as per recommendations.
    *   Ensure adequate application of **Potassium**, as its deficiency often manifests as scorching of leaf margins.
*   **Weed Management:**
    *   Implement effective weed control measures to reduce competition for water and nutrients, which can exacerbate stress.
*   **Herbicide Application:**
    *   Use recommended herbicides at correct dosages and application methods.
    *   Avoid spraying herbicides during high temperatures, strong winds, or when plants are under stress, to prevent phytotoxicity.
*   **Trash Mulching:**
    *   Retaining sugarcane trash (residue) as mulch in the field can help conserve soil moisture, moderate soil temperature, and reduce weed growth, thereby mitigating some environmental stress factors.
