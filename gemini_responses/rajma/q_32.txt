Drying up of branches, leaves, fruits, and flowers in Rajma (Kidney Bean) can be a symptom of various underlying issues, including fungal diseases, bacterial diseases, insect pests, nematodes, and physiological stresses. A precise diagnosis is crucial for effective management.

Here's a breakdown of potential causes and their scientifically verified management strategies relevant to Haryana:

### 1. Fungal Diseases

Fungal pathogens often cause wilting and drying by affecting the vascular system or root/stem tissues.

*   **Fusarium Wilt / Rhizoctonia Root Rot:**
    *   **Symptoms:** Yellowing of lower leaves, wilting, stunting, drying of entire plant. In Fusarium wilt, vascular discoloration (brownish streaks) can be observed in the stem. Rhizoctonia causes reddish-brown lesions at the collar region, leading to damping-off in seedlings or root rot in older plants.
    *   **Management:**
        *   **Cultural Practices:** Use resistant varieties, practice crop rotation with non-host crops (e.g., cereals), ensure good drainage, and avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization.
        *   **Chemical Control:**
            *   **Seed Treatment:** Treat seeds with a combination of **Carbendazim** 50 WP at 1 g/kg seed + **Thiram** 75 WS at 2 g/kg seed.
            *   **Soil Drench (for established plants):** If symptoms appear, drench the soil around affected plants with **Carbendazim** 50 WP at 1 g/L water, applying 200-250 ml solution per plant.

*   **Sclerotinia Stem Rot (White Mold):**
    *   **Symptoms:** Water-soaked lesions on stems, branches, or pods, often covered with white, cottony fungal growth. Black, hard sclerotia (resting bodies) may be visible. Affected parts wilt and dry up.
    *   **Management:**
        *   **Cultural Practices:** Ensure proper plant spacing for good air circulation, remove and destroy infected plant debris, deep ploughing to bury sclerotia.
        *   **Chemical Control:**
            *   **Foliar Spray:** Apply **Carbendazim** 50 WP at 0.5 g/L water or **Thiophanate methyl** 70 WP at 0.7 g/L water at the appearance of symptoms and repeat after 10-15 days if necessary.

### 2. Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial pathogens can cause blights that lead to drying of foliage and other plant parts.

*   **Common Bacterial Blight / Halo Blight:**
    *   **Symptoms:** Water-soaked spots on leaves that enlarge, turn necrotic (brown and dry), and are often surrounded by a narrow yellow border (Common Bacterial Blight) or a wide yellow halo (Halo Blight). Pods may also develop water-soaked spots that become sunken and reddish-brown. Severe infection leads to premature defoliation and drying.
    *   **Management:**
        *   **Cultural Practices:** Use disease-free certified seeds, practice crop rotation, avoid overhead irrigation, and destroy infected plant debris.
        *   **Chemical Control:**
            *   **Seed Treatment:** Hot water treatment (50Â°C for 30 minutes) can reduce seed-borne inoculum.
            *   **Foliar Spray:** Apply a mixture of **Streptomycin sulphate** 90% + **Tetracycline hydrochloride** 10% (antibiotic mixture) at 0.5 g/L water, or **Copper oxychloride** 50 WP at 2 g/L water, at the first sign of symptoms. Repeat at 10-15 day intervals.

### 3. Insect Pests

Certain insect pests can cause wilting and drying, especially by damaging the stem or roots.

*   **Stem Fly (Ophiomyia phaseoli):**
    *   **Symptoms:** Primarily affects young plants. Larvae bore into the stem, causing wilting, yellowing, stunting, and eventually drying up of the plant. A characteristic swelling at the base of the stem may be observed.
    *   **Management:**
        *   **Cultural Practices:** Early sowing can sometimes help plants establish before peak pest activity.
        *   **Chemical Control:**
            *   **Seed Treatment:** Treat seeds with **Thiamethoxam** 70 WS at 3 g/kg seed or **Imidacloprid** 70 WS at 5 g/kg seed.
            *   **Foliar Spray (early stage):** If infestation is observed in young plants, spray with **Dimethoate** 30 EC at 1.7 ml/L water or **Chlorantraniliprole** 18.5 SC at 0.3 ml/L water.

### 4. Nematodes

Nematodes can damage roots, impairing water and nutrient uptake, leading to wilting and drying.

*   **Root-Knot Nematodes (Meloidogyne spp.):**
    *   **Symptoms:** Stunted growth, yellowing, wilting, and premature drying of plants, even with adequate moisture. Characteristic galls (swellings) on the roots are a definitive sign.
    *   **Management:**
        *   **Cultural Practices:** Practice crop rotation with non-host crops (e.g., marigold, cereals), use resistant varieties if available, and incorporate organic matter into the soil.
        *   **Chemical Control:**
            *   **Soil Application:** Apply **Carbofuran** 3G at 10 kg/acre by broadcasting and incorporating into the soil before sowing.
            *   **Bio-nematicides:** Use formulations of *Paecilomyces lilacinus* or *Purpureocillium lilacinum* as per product recommendations for soil application.

### 5. Physiological Stress

Environmental factors can also lead to drying symptoms.

*   **Water Stress (Drought or Waterlogging):**
    *   **Symptoms:** Wilting, scorching of leaf margins, and eventual drying of leaves and entire plants. Both insufficient water and excessive water (leading to root rot due to lack of oxygen) can cause similar symptoms.
    *   **Management:** Maintain optimal soil moisture through proper irrigation scheduling. Ensure good drainage to prevent waterlogging.

*   **Nutrient Deficiencies:**
    *   **Symptoms:** Specific nutrient deficiencies (e.g., potassium, phosphorus) can lead to marginal scorching, necrosis, and drying of leaves.
    *   **Management:** Conduct a soil test to identify deficiencies and apply balanced fertilizers as per recommendations.

*   **Heat Stress:**
    *   **Symptoms:** Scorching and drying of leaves, flowers, and young pods, especially during hot, dry winds.
    *   **Management:** Ensure adequate irrigation during hot periods. Consider appropriate sowing times to avoid peak heat stress during critical growth stages.

**Important Note:** Always confirm the specific cause of drying up through careful observation of symptoms or by consulting with a local agricultural expert or the HAU extension services for accurate diagnosis and tailored recommendations. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) approaches, combining cultural, biological, and chemical methods, are most effective for sustainable control.
