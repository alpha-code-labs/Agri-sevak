### Maturity Signs for Wheat

Physiological maturity in wheat is reached when the grain has accumulated its maximum dry weight. This is indicated by several visual cues:

*   **Loss of Green Colour:** The most prominent sign is the complete yellowing or straw colouration of the leaves, stem, and glumes (chaff). The entire plant turns from green to golden yellow.
*   **Hardening of Grains:** The grains transition from a milky stage to a soft dough, then to a hard dough stage. When pressed between the thumb and forefinger, the grain should be hard and not exude any milky sap.
*   **Black Layer Formation:** A dark spot or "black layer" may be visible at the base of the kernel where it attaches to the rachis. This indicates the cessation of nutrient and water flow to the grain.
*   **Moisture Content:** At physiological maturity, the grain moisture content typically ranges from 20-25%. However, harvesting usually occurs at a lower moisture content for optimal storage and processing.

### Harvesting Time for Wheat

The optimal harvesting time is crucial to minimize grain loss and ensure good quality.

*   **Grain Moisture Content:** The primary indicator for harvesting is the grain moisture content.
    *   For **manual harvesting**, wheat is typically harvested when the grain moisture content is around 15-18%.
    *   For **mechanical harvesting (combine harvesters)**, the ideal grain moisture content is 12-14%. Harvesting at higher moisture content can lead to spoilage, while harvesting at much lower moisture content can result in shattering losses and grain breakage.
*   **Visual Cues:** The crop should be uniformly golden yellow, and the grains should be hard and dry.
*   **Typical Period in Haryana:** In Haryana, wheat harvesting generally commences from late March and continues through April, depending on the sowing time and variety.
*   **Weather Conditions:** Harvesting should ideally be done during dry weather to prevent grain spoilage and facilitate efficient threshing. Avoid harvesting immediately after rain.

### Cutting Process for Wheat

The cutting process for wheat in Haryana primarily involves two methods: manual and mechanical.

*   **1. Manual Harvesting:**
    *   **Tool:** Farmers use a **sickle** to cut the wheat plants.
    *   **Process:** Labourers cut the plants close to the ground (typically leaving a stubble height of 10-15 cm). The cut plants are then gathered into bundles (sheaves) and left in the field for a few days to dry further.
    *   **Threshing:** After drying, these bundles are transported to a threshing floor or a stationary thresher for separation of grain from straw.
    *   **Advantages:** Lower initial cost, suitable for small landholdings, allows for collection of longer straw for fodder.
    *   **Disadvantages:** Labour-intensive, time-consuming, higher risk of weather damage if harvesting is prolonged.

*   **2. Mechanical Harvesting:**
    *   **Tools:**
        *   **Combine Harvesters:** These are the most common mechanical harvesters in Haryana. They perform cutting, threshing, and cleaning operations in a single pass. Both self-propelled and tractor-mounted combines are used.
        *   **Reaper-Binders:** Less common now, these machines cut the crop and tie it into bundles, which are then threshed separately using a thresher.
    *   **Process (Combine Harvester):**
        *   The combine's header cuts the standing crop at a desired height (adjustable, typically 15-30 cm, but can be lower).
        *   The cut material is fed into the machine, where the threshing cylinder separates the grain from the straw.
        *   The grain is cleaned by sieves and a fan, then collected in a grain tank.
        *   The straw and chaff are discharged from the rear of the machine, either spread evenly or windrowed.
    *   **Advantages:** Highly efficient, significantly reduces labour and harvesting time, minimizes post-harvest losses if operated correctly.
    *   **Disadvantages:** Higher initial investment, requires skilled operators, leaves stubble in the field which can be a challenge for residue management (leading to stubble burning if not managed properly).

It is crucial to ensure proper adjustment of combine harvesters (e.g., cutting height, reel speed, cylinder speed, concave clearance, fan speed) to minimize grain losses and damage during mechanical harvesting.
