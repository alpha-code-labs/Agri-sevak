### Poplar (Populus) Maturity Signs, Harvesting Time, and Cutting Process

Poplar cultivation in Haryana is primarily for timber, plywood, matchsticks, and pulpwood. Unlike agricultural crops with biological maturity (e.g., fruit ripening), Poplar's "maturity" is determined by its silvicultural and economic readiness for harvest, based on achieving optimal size and wood quality for its intended end-use.

#### 1. Maturity Signs

The primary indicators for Poplar maturity are:

*   **Attainment of Target Dimensions:** The most crucial sign is when the trees reach the desired diameter at breast height (DBH) and height suitable for the intended market. For plywood and timber, this typically means a DBH of 30 cm or more.
*   **Age:** While age is a factor, it's secondary to size. In Haryana's agroforestry systems, Poplar is generally harvested at:
    *   **5-7 years:** For pulpwood, matchwood, or smaller diameter timber.
    *   **7-10 years (or up to 12 years):** For high-quality plywood and saw timber, allowing for larger log sizes and better economic returns.
*   **Culmination of Mean Annual Increment (MAI):** From a silvicultural perspective, trees are mature when their growth rate (volume increment) starts to decline, indicating that the tree is no longer adding significant economic value in terms of volume growth. This is often observed as a slowing down of diameter and height growth.
*   **Wood Quality:** The wood should have developed the desired characteristics (e.g., density, straightness, absence of major defects) suitable for its end product.

#### 2. Harvesting Time

The optimal time for harvesting Poplar in Haryana is during the dormant season:

*   **Seasonal Timing:** Harvesting is best conducted from **late autumn to early spring (typically November to March)**. During this period, the trees have shed their leaves, sap flow is minimal, and the wood moisture content is lower, which facilitates felling, handling, and reduces the risk of wood splitting.
*   **Rotation Length:** As mentioned under maturity signs, the specific harvesting age depends on the end-use:
    *   **Short Rotation (5-7 years):** For pulpwood, matchwood, or smaller diameter logs.
    *   **Medium Rotation (7-10 years):** For plywood and general timber, aiming for larger girth and higher value.

#### 3. Cutting Process

The cutting process involves felling the tree, delimbing, and bucking, with a strong emphasis on safety.

*   **Safety First:** Always ensure the use of appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including a helmet, eye protection, ear protection, gloves, safety boots, and chainsaw chaps. Ensure trained personnel operate chainsaws.
*   **Tools:**
    *   **Chainsaw:** The primary tool for felling and bucking.
    *   **Felling Wedges:** Used to assist in directional felling and prevent the saw from pinching.
    *   **Measuring Tape:** For accurate log length measurement.
*   **Felling Technique:**
    *   **Directional Felling:** Before cutting, assess the tree's lean, surrounding obstacles, and plan the felling direction to ensure safety and ease of extraction. Clear the area around the tree.
    *   **Notch Cut (Undercut):** Make a wedge-shaped cut on the side of the tree in the intended direction of fall. This typically involves:
        *   A horizontal cut (bottom cut) made to about 1/3rd of the tree's diameter.
        *   An angled cut (top cut) meeting the horizontal cut to remove a wedge of wood.
    *   **Back Cut (Felling Cut):** Made on the opposite side of the notch cut, slightly above the horizontal cut of the notch. This cut is made towards the notch, leaving a hinge of uncut wood.
    *   **Hinge:** The uncut wood between the notch and the back cut. It guides the tree's fall and prevents it from splitting or kicking back. The hinge should be uniform in thickness.
    *   **Stump Height:** Cut the tree as close to the ground as practically possible, typically leaving a stump of **10-15 cm** above ground level, to maximize timber volume and facilitate subsequent land preparation.
*   **Limbing and Bucking:**
    *   **Limbing:** Once the tree is felled, remove all branches from the main stem. Start from the base and work towards the top, ensuring stability of the log.
    *   **Bucking:** Cut the main stem into desired log lengths based on market specifications (e.g., **2.5 m, 3.0 m, 4.0 m** for plywood, or shorter lengths for pulpwood). Measure accurately to avoid wastage.
*   **Extraction:** Transport the cut logs from the felling site to a loading point. This can be done manually, using animal power, or with mechanical means like tractors or skidders, depending on the scale of operation and terrain.

By following these guidelines, growers can ensure efficient and safe harvesting of Poplar, maximizing their economic returns.
