As a Senior Agronomist at Haryana Agricultural University (HAU, Hisar), I understand your concern regarding viral diseases in Onion. It is crucial to understand that once a plant is infected with a virus, there is no direct chemical cure. Therefore, control strategies primarily focus on prevention and managing the vectors (insects) that transmit these viruses.

Let's address the specific conditions you mentioned:

*   **Tristeza:** This is not known to affect Onion.

For other viral diseases like Leaf curl, Mosaic, Yellow vein clearing, Stripe, and Stunt virus, these are often symptoms caused by common onion viruses such as **Onion Yellow Dwarf Virus (OYDV)** and **Iris Yellow Spot Virus (IYSV)**, which are prevalent in our region. OYDV is primarily transmitted by aphids, while IYSV is transmitted by thrips.

Here's a comprehensive approach to manage viral diseases in Onion:

**I. Preventive Measures (Most Crucial):**

*   **Use Healthy Planting Material:** Always use virus-free bulbs, sets, or seeds from certified sources. This is the most fundamental step to prevent initial infection.
*   **Resistant Varieties:** If available, choose onion varieties known to have resistance or tolerance to common onion viruses. Consult with HAU experts for local recommendations.
*   **Crop Rotation:** Practice crop rotation with non-host crops to break the disease cycle and reduce inoculum build-up in the soil.
*   **Isolation:** Avoid planting new onion crops adjacent to older, potentially infected onion fields or other susceptible host plants.

**II. Vector Management (Controlling Insect Pests that Spread Viruses):**

Since aphids and thrips are the primary vectors, their timely control is essential.

*   **Regular Monitoring:** Regularly inspect your onion fields for the presence of aphids and thrips, especially on young leaves and growing points.
*   **Cultural Practices:**
    *   **Weed Control:** Keep the field and surrounding areas free of weeds, as many weeds can act as alternate hosts for both viruses and their insect vectors.
    *   **Reflective Mulches:** Using silver or reflective plastic mulches can deter aphids and thrips from landing on plants, especially during early growth stages.
*   **Chemical Control (Insecticides for Vectors):** Apply insecticides as soon as vectors are observed, following recommended dosages and safety precautions. Rotate insecticides with different modes of action to prevent resistance development.

    *   **For Aphids (OYDV vector):**
        *   **Imidacloprid** 17.8% SL: 125-150 ml/acre (foliar spray)
        *   **Thiamethoxam** 25% WG: 80-100 g/acre (foliar spray)
        *   **Acetamiprid** 20% SP: 80-100 g/acre (foliar spray)

    *   **For Thrips (IYSV vector):**
        *   **Fipronil** 5% SC: 400-600 ml/acre (foliar spray)
        *   **Spirotetramat** 150 OD: 200-250 ml/acre (foliar spray)
        *   **Cyantraniliprole** 10.26% OD: 180 ml/acre (foliar spray)
        *   **Emamectin Benzoate** 5% SG: 80-100 g/acre (foliar spray)

**III. Field Sanitation:**

*   **Rogueing:** Promptly remove and destroy (bury or burn away from the field) any plants showing symptoms of viral infection. This prevents the spread of the virus to healthy plants.
*   **Destroy Crop Residues:** After harvest, remove and destroy all onion crop residues to eliminate potential sources of inoculum and overwintering vectors.

By implementing these integrated management strategies, you can significantly reduce the incidence and impact of viral diseases in your onion crop. Always consult with your local agricultural extension officer or HAU experts for specific recommendations tailored to your farm conditions.
