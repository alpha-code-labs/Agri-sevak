As a Senior Agronomist at Haryana Agricultural University (HAU), Hisar, I understand the importance of maintaining optimal nutrient balance for healthy Fig (Anjeer) cultivation. Toxicity from excessive levels of micronutrients like Zinc (Zn) and Manganese (Mn), or macronutrients like Iron (Fe) and Magnesium (Mg), can severely impact plant health and yield.

Before implementing any management strategy, it is **crucial to confirm the toxicity** through comprehensive soil and leaf tissue analysis from a reputable laboratory, such as those at HAU. Visual symptoms can often be misleading and may resemble other nutrient deficiencies or stresses.

Here's how to manage excessive levels of these nutrients in Fig:

### General Management Principles:
1.  **Soil and Leaf Analysis:** Always start with a diagnostic test to confirm the specific nutrient toxicity and its severity.
2.  **Identify and Eliminate Source:** Determine if the excess is due to over-fertilization, contaminated irrigation water, or specific soil conditions. Stop or reduce the application of the problematic nutrient.
3.  **Improve Soil Health:** Enhance soil structure, drainage, and organic matter content to improve nutrient buffering and availability.

### Specific Management Strategies:

#### 1. Zinc (Zn) Toxicity
Excessive Zinc can lead to chlorosis (yellowing) of younger leaves, stunted growth, reddish-brown spots, and can induce Iron deficiency.
*   **Causes:** Over-application of Zn fertilizers, acidic soils (pH < 6.0), or contaminated irrigation water.
*   **Management:**
    *   **Stop Zn application:** Immediately cease any fertilizer or foliar spray containing Zinc.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** Apply **Calcium carbonate** (agricultural lime) or **Dolomite** (calcium magnesium carbonate) to raise the soil pH to 6.0-7.0. This reduces Zn availability.
        *   **Calcium carbonate:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, depending on current pH and soil type, as per soil test recommendations.
        *   **Dolomite:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, if Magnesium levels are also low or adequate.
    *   **Add Organic Matter:** Incorporate well-decomposed Farmyard Manure (FYM) or compost at 10-20 kg/plant (for mature trees) or 5-10 tonnes/acre. Organic matter chelates Zn, making it less available to plants.
    *   **Improve Drainage:** Ensure good soil drainage to prevent waterlogging.

#### 2. Iron (Fe) Toxicity
Iron toxicity (often called "bronzing") typically manifests as bronzing or purpling of leaves, small dark spots, and necrosis, especially in waterlogged or highly acidic conditions.
*   **Causes:** Highly acidic soils (pH < 5.5), prolonged waterlogging, or excessive Fe fertilization.
*   **Management:**
    *   **Stop Fe application:** Discontinue any Iron-containing fertilizers or foliar sprays.
    *   **Improve Drainage and Aeration:** This is critical. Ensure proper drainage to prevent waterlogging, which increases soluble Fe2+. Improve soil aeration through tillage or adding organic matter.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** Apply **Calcium carbonate** or **Dolomite** to raise the soil pH to 6.0-7.0. This converts soluble Fe2+ to less available Fe3+.
        *   **Calcium carbonate:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, as per soil test recommendations.
        *   **Dolomite:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, if Magnesium levels are also low or adequate.
    *   **Add Organic Matter:** Incorporate well-decomposed FYM or compost at 10-20 kg/plant or 5-10 tonnes/acre to improve soil structure and aeration.

#### 3. Magnesium (Mg) Toxicity
Magnesium toxicity is less common but can occur. High Mg levels can antagonize the uptake of Calcium (Ca) and Potassium (K), leading to their deficiencies and potentially poor soil structure.
*   **Causes:** Over-application of Mg fertilizers, high Mg content in irrigation water, or naturally high Mg soils.
*   **Management:**
    *   **Stop Mg application:** Cease any Magnesium-containing fertilizers.
    *   **Increase Calcium Levels:** Apply **Gypsum** (calcium sulfate) to increase Calcium in the soil, which can help balance high Magnesium and improve soil structure.
        *   **Gypsum:** Apply 500-1000 kg/acre, especially if soil structure is poor or sodium levels are also high.
    *   **Leaching:** If the soil is well-drained, leach the soil with good quality irrigation water to wash out excess soluble Mg.
    *   **Ensure Adequate Potassium:** Maintain optimal Potassium levels as K can antagonize Mg uptake.

#### 4. Manganese (Mn) Toxicity
Manganese toxicity causes chlorosis, crinkling or cupping of leaves, dark spots (often purplish-brown or black) on older leaves, and marginal necrosis. It can also induce Iron deficiency.
*   **Causes:** Highly acidic soils (pH < 5.5), waterlogging, or excessive Mn fertilization.
*   **Management:**
    *   **Stop Mn application:** Discontinue any Manganese-containing fertilizers or foliar sprays.
    *   **Increase Soil pH:** This is the most effective method. Apply **Calcium carbonate** or **Dolomite** to raise the soil pH to 6.0-7.0. Higher pH significantly reduces Mn availability.
        *   **Calcium carbonate:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, as per soil test recommendations.
        *   **Dolomite:** Apply 500-1500 kg/acre, if Magnesium levels are also low or adequate.
    *   **Improve Drainage and Aeration:** Prevent waterlogging, which increases soluble Mn. Improve soil aeration.
    *   **Add Organic Matter:** Incorporate well-decomposed FYM or compost at 10-20 kg/plant or 5-10 tonnes/acre to improve soil structure and buffering capacity.

Remember, balanced nutrition is key to healthy Fig cultivation. Always consult with HAU experts for specific recommendations tailored to your farm's soil conditions.
