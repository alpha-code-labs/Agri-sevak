As a Senior Agronomist at Haryana Agricultural University (HAU, Hisar), I understand your concern regarding viral diseases in tomato. It's crucial to remember that once a plant is infected with a virus, there is no direct chemical cure. Therefore, management strategies primarily focus on prevention and controlling the vectors (insects) that transmit these viruses.

First, let's address one of the viruses you mentioned:

*   **Tristeza:** This is not known to affect Tomato. Citrus Tristeza Virus (CTV) primarily affects citrus species.

For the other viral diseases you mentioned (Leaf curl, Mosaic, Yellow vein clearing, Stripe, and Stunt virus), which are significant threats to tomato cultivation in Haryana, here's a comprehensive control strategy:

**General Principles for Viral Disease Management:**

1.  **No Cure:** Once a plant is infected, it cannot be cured. The focus must be on preventing infection and removing infected plants to prevent further spread.
2.  **Vector Control:** Most tomato viruses are transmitted by insect vectors like whiteflies, aphids, and thrips. Controlling these vectors is paramount.
3.  **Sanitation:** Removing infected plants and weeds (which can act as alternate hosts) is critical.

**Specific Control Measures:**

1.  **Use of Resistant/Tolerant Varieties:**
    *   This is the most effective and sustainable long-term solution. Always choose tomato varieties that are known to be resistant or tolerant to common viruses like Tomato Leaf Curl Virus (ToLCV) prevalent in Haryana. Consult with HAU or local agricultural extension for recommended varieties.

2.  **Vector Management (Insect Control):**
    *   **Whiteflies (Vector for Tomato Leaf Curl Virus - ToLCV, which causes leaf curl and yellow vein clearing):**
        *   **Nursery Stage:** Treat seeds or drench nursery beds with systemic insecticides to protect young seedlings.
            *   **Imidacloprid** 17.8% SL: 0.5 ml/L of water for drenching.
            *   **Thiamethoxam** 25% WG: 0.2 g/L of water for drenching.
        *   **Field Stage:** Regular monitoring and timely application of insecticides are crucial. Rotate insecticides with different modes of action to prevent resistance development.
            *   **Imidacloprid** 17.8% SL: 0.5-0.75 ml/L of water (100-150 ml/acre).
            *   **Thiamethoxam** 25% WG: 0.2 g/L of water (80 g/acre).
            *   **Acetamiprid** 20% SP: 0.2 g/L of water (80-100 g/acre).
            *   **Spiromesifen** 22.9% SC: 0.5 ml/L of water (200 ml/acre).
            *   **Buprofezin** 25% SC: 1.25 ml/L of water (500 ml/acre).
    *   **Aphids (Vector for Cucumber Mosaic Virus - CMV, which causes mosaic and stunting):**
        *   **Imidacloprid** 17.8% SL: 0.5-0.75 ml/L of water (100-150 ml/acre).
        *   **Thiamethoxam** 25% WG: 0.2 g/L of water (80 g/acre).
        *   **Flonicamid** 50% WG: 0.3 g/L of water (120-150 g/acre).
    *   **Thrips (Vector for Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus - TSWV, which can cause stripe and stunting symptoms):**
        *   **Imidacloprid** 17.8% SL: 0.5-0.75 ml/L of water (100-150 ml/acre).
        *   **Thiamethoxam** 25% WG: 0.2 g/L of water (80 g/acre).
        *   **Fipronil** 5% SC: 1.5-2 ml/L of water (600-800 ml/acre).
        *   **Spinosad** 45% SC: 0.25 ml/L of water (100 ml/acre).

3.  **Cultural Practices:**
    *   **Nursery Management:** Raise seedlings in insect-proof net houses (40-50 mesh nylon net) to protect them from early vector infestation.
    *   **Removal of Infected Plants (Roguing):** Regularly inspect your field and immediately uproot and destroy (bury or burn away from the field) any plants showing viral symptoms. This prevents the virus from spreading to healthy plants.
    *   **Weed Control:** Keep the field and surrounding areas free of weeds, as many weeds act as alternate hosts for viruses and their insect vectors.
    *   **Crop Rotation:** Avoid planting tomato or other solanaceous crops (like brinjal, chilli, potato) continuously in the same field. Rotate with non-host crops.
    *   **Field Sanitation:** Remove all crop debris after harvest.
    *   **Use of Healthy Seedlings:** Always use healthy, certified, virus-free seedlings from a reliable source.
    *   **Mulching:** Use silver or reflective plastic mulches in the field. These mulches repel whiteflies and aphids, reducing vector landing on plants.
    *   **Nutrient Management:** Ensure balanced nutrition for plants to improve their vigor and tolerance to stress, though it won't prevent viral infection.
    *   **Avoid Mechanical Transmission:** For viruses like Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV), which can be mechanically transmitted, avoid handling plants when wet, and sanitize tools (e.g., with 10% bleach solution) between plants. Avoid using tobacco products while working in the field, as tobacco can carry ToMV.

By adopting an integrated approach combining resistant varieties, strict vector control, and diligent cultural practices, you can significantly reduce the incidence and impact of viral diseases in your tomato crop. For specific recommendations tailored to your local conditions and current pest pressure, please consult with the experts at HAU or your local Krishi Vigyan Kendra (KVK).
